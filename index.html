About

The designer
behind the work

I started in product design, working with physical materials and tangible objects. But somewhere along the way I realised that the real leverage was not in designing a single touchpoint but in understanding the whole system behind it, every process, every handoff, every moment where something could go wrong for a real person. That shift is what pulled me into service design, and into the belief that how institutions work, or fail to work, shapes people's lives in ways that are rarely visible until something breaks.

That same conviction is what brought me to the UK and to UAL. Coming from Peru, where institutions carry enormous weight in people's lives and often fail them, I wanted to work where public service design is taken seriously as a practice. The goal has always been to bring that back.

For me, good service design starts with getting everyone in the room. Co-designing with users, stakeholders and process experts is not just good practice, it is how you build the trust and shared understanding that makes change actually stick. And once you have that, the most valuable thing I do is reframe. Helping organisations question their own assumptions, uncover where the real problem is hiding, and plan a strategy around that. That is where design earns its place.

01
Declaration of Health
Royal London
+
Context

Royal London is the UK's largest mutual life insurance company. Protection customers manage their policies through independent financial advisers, which means Royal London often has limited direct contact with end customers. This project sat within the protection servicing team and focused on the communications customers receive when their policy is at risk of lapsing.

Problem

When a customer misses payments and their policy lapses, Royal London sends a Declaration of Health form to reinstate it. The problem came to light through a complaint: a customer had just been diagnosed with cancer. In the stress of that, she missed an automatic payment, her policy lapsed, and when she tried to reinstate it she completed the declaration honestly. Because of her diagnosis, we could not take her on again. The process had worked exactly as designed. But it had failed her completely. The real issue was the communication journey leading up to it. By the time customers reached the form, it was already too late — they had arrived with no warning, no support and no real understanding of what was happening to them.

Design Process
  • Research and iteration: Ran 5 rounds of qualitative testing with 24 participants total through UserTesting, using the actual letters as artefacts to anchor conversations. Participants included customers who had experienced financial hardship, serious illness or bereavement, so we could understand how the communications landed in the moments they were most needed.
  • Synthesis: Each round revealed where the tone was missing, where the information was unclear, and where customers felt abandoned rather than supported. Customers were reaching the Declaration of Health with no warning and no support. The problem started long before they ever saw the form.
  • Stakeholder alignment: Facilitated feedback sessions with marketing, subject matter experts and vulnerable customer panels to pressure-test tone, clarity and accessibility across the full journey.
Outcomes
3-stage communication journey replacing single notice 24 vulnerable customers across 5 rounds of qualitative testing Helping Hand resources integrated across all lapse communications Clear payment update instructions added to reduce friction Designs handed over to implementation team at project close
Solution

Redesigned the full communication journey for lapsing policies. Added a new reminder letter 20 days after the first notice, creating a 3-stage arc: warning at 35 days, reminder at 20 days, confirmation of cancellation. All three letters now include signposting to Helping Hand, Royal London's free wellbeing service, with resources for financial support, mental health and medical help. QR codes were added for easy mobile access. Instructions for updating payment methods were simplified and made prominent throughout.

Takeaways

The most valuable thing I did on this project was to reframe the challenge from a form redesign problem to a communication project. We found quickly that the gap was in how we communicated before the form was ever needed. This reframe came directly from listening to customers, and it changed the entire direction of the work.

02
Agricultural Payrolls
Interbank
+
Context

Every harvest season, an agricultural company in La Libertad, Peru brings in hundreds of seasonal workers. Each one needed a bank account to receive their paycheck. Interbank was the selected bank by their employers, but the process was broken: long queues under the sun, difficulty validating identities for workers with worn fingerprints or who could not read, and at the end of it all, workers throwing their debit cards away because they had no idea what to do with them after receiving their wage.

Problem

The client framing was a usability problem: how do we make the account opening process faster? But when we got to the field, it became clear the real problem was different. Most workers had no interest in a bank account. They wanted their money, in cash, that day. The debit card was an obstacle, not a solution.

Design Process
  • Research phase: Travelled to La Libertad for a 2-day field research sprint. Conducted interviews and shadowing with workers ranging from 21-year-old college students to 56-year-old illiterate seasonal labourers, observing the full payroll journey from queue to withdrawal.
  • Synthesis: The key insight was that almost every worker had a mobile phone. And Interbank already had a cardless ATM withdrawal service. The question shifted from "how do we onboard them to banking?" to "what if there is no card at all?"
  • Prototype and test: Built three prototypes later that first day: a tablet app, a USSD mobile service and a mock ATM cardless withdrawal flow. Tested all three with users on day two, including workers who had never used digital banking. After receiving overall positive feedback we returned to Lima to iterate and gather a work team to make this idea happen.
Outcomes
Reframed from digital onboarding to cardless payment service 3 prototypes built and tested with real users in under 48 hours Solution designed for users ranging from digitally confident to illiterate Proposed service blueprint adopted as basis for pilot in Trujillo
Solution

Proposed a service redesign centred on USSD protocols and Interbank's existing cardless withdrawal infrastructure. Workers could check their balance and generate a withdrawal code from any basic mobile phone, no smartphone required, no debit card, no queue. The solution also added transparent payroll notifications so workers knew exactly how much they had earned before payday, something that had never existed before.

Takeaways

This is the project where I understood what service design really meant for me. The moment we asked "what if there is no card?" everything changed. The answer was already in the system, we just needed to look at it from the worker's perspective first. Designing for people with low digital literacy also taught me that simplicity takes real effort. It means understanding what someone actually needs, in their actual context, and stripping everything else away.

03
MA Project
NHS Pioneer Centre
+
Context

NHS Pioneer Wound Healing and Lymphoedema Centres provide specialist aftercare to patients with chronic, complex conditions. Lymphoedema is a lifelong condition with no cure, managed through daily self-care routines, compression garments and regular clinic visits. Patients are often vulnerable, elderly or managing multiple conditions simultaneously. This was my MA major project at UAL.

Problem

Despite excellent clinical care and strong patient reviews, the service had a significant gap: once patients finished their treatment programme, there was no structured support to help them manage their condition independently and avoid relapse. The clinic also lacked a clear strategy for the future. There were ideas on the table, including exploring VR, but no defined direction or roadmap to evaluate them against. The challenge was both immediate and long-term: how do you support patients after they leave, and how do you build a coherent vision for where the service needs to go?

Design Process
  • Research phase: Conducted qualitative research with lymphoedema and wound care patients, nurses and NHS staff to map the current aftercare journey and understand where the system was failing patients between touchpoints.
  • Synthesis: Used systems mapping to identify the feedback loops driving deterioration, and anticipatory innovation methods to explore what future scenarios for NHS capacity and patient self-management might look like in 5–10 years.
  • Strategy development: Developed a strategic roadmap using design futures methodology, mapping signals of change to identify which near and mid-term actions would best position the service for preferred futures, rather than designing a single intervention.
  • Co-design: Facilitated workshops with clinical staff and patients to validate the direction and stress-test proposals against the realities of NHS delivery.
Outcomes
Full service blueprint mapping current and future state Strategic roadmap using anticipatory innovation methods Co-designed patient education materials Presented to NHS Pioneer Centre clinical team
Solution

A strategic roadmap with phased interventions prioritising patient self-management capability, including a digital support tool for between-appointment guidance, clearer discharge and re-referral pathways, and a co-designed patient education resource developed with input from people living with the conditions.

Takeaways

Working with patients who are managing lifelong conditions taught me a lot about what it means to design for dignity as well as function. The anticipatory innovation approach was genuinely useful here because the NHS cannot afford to design only for today. You have to understand where things are going and design for that future, not the current state. I would take that lens to any complex public sector challenge.

I enjoy using AI tools for creative coding and prototyping ideas quickly. One of the projects I am most proud of is my wedding website. I wanted it to feel personal rather than generic, and I did not want to rely on external services for livestreaming, gift management or photo sharing. It also needed to be genuinely useful, almost all of my guests are from Peru, so explaining transportation, weather, accommodation and logistics in Scotland required more than a single page.

Built from scratch using AI, HTML, CSS and JavaScript, deployed on GitHub Pages with a Google Sheets backend for form responses. No templates, no shortcuts. Bilingual, responsive, with custom animations and live forms.
